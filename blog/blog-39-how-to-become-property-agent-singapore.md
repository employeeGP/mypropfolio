# How to Become a Property Agent in Singapore: Step-by-Step Guide (2026)

Singapore's property market moves SGD 30+ billion in residential transactions every year. With over 36,800 registered property agents, it is one of the most active agent communities in Asia — and one of the most competitive. For those who build their knowledge, systems, and client base systematically, it can also be one of the most rewarding careers in the country.

This guide covers everything you need to know to enter the profession in 2026: eligibility requirements, the RES examination process, CEA registration, agency selection, and what the first 12 months actually look like.

---

## Step 1: Check Your Eligibility

Before signing up for any course, confirm that you meet the CEA's entry criteria.

**Age:** You must be at least 21 years old.

**Citizenship/Residency:** <cite index="28-1">You must be a Singaporean or Singapore Permanent Resident (SPR) for at least 10 years. If you are an SPR who does not fulfil the 10-year criteria, you need to submit either a Certificate of No Criminal Conviction (CNCC) from your country of origin, or supporting documents that prove you have lived in Singapore for at least 10 years.</cite>

Foreigners with valid Employment Passes can also enter the profession, but must go through an estate agency to seek a CEA preliminary registration eligibility assessment.

**Education:** <cite index="21-1">You must have a minimum of 4 'O' level passes or equivalent to take the RES examination.</cite> Overseas or private qualifications require a CEA assessment before they are accepted.

**Character:** <cite index="21-1">To be deemed fit and proper to be a real estate agent, you must not have past issues involving fraud, dishonesty, breach of trust, or serious criminal matters. You also must not be an undischarged bankrupt, and you must declare all previous convictions, including those from other countries.</cite>

If you meet all four criteria, you can proceed to the RES course.

---

## Step 2: Complete the RES Course

<cite index="23-1">You must attend and complete the real estate salespersons (RES) course conducted by a CEA Approved Course Provider (ACP) before registering for the RES examination. A minimum of 75% attendance is required to receive the Certificate of RES Course Completion.</cite>

The RES course covers:
- Singapore's property laws and regulations
- Property agency work and ethics
- Housing policies (HDB, private residential, commercial)
- Property transaction processes
- CEA's Code of Ethics and Professional Client Care

**Duration:** Typically 1-2 months depending on whether you choose weekday or weekend classes.

**Cost:** Approximately SGD 700-1,200 depending on the course provider. SkillsFuture credits can be used to offset fees.

**CEA Approved Course Providers (2026):** Multiple providers including NTUC LearningHub, Kaplan, and others. <cite index="26-1">As of 1 Jan 2026, Institute of Estate Agents, Singapore is no longer a CEA Approved Course Provider.</cite> Always verify current providers on the CEA website before enrolling.

**NUS/NP graduates:** <cite index="23-1">Holders of the Bachelor of Business Administration (Real Estate) from NUS are exempted from Paper 1. Holders of Bachelor of Science (Real Estate) from NUS and Diploma in Real Estate Business from NP who enrolled on or after 1 April 2020 are also exempted from Paper 1.</cite>

The Certificate of RES Course Completion is valid for **2 years**. You must pass the exam within this window — otherwise, you need to retake the course.

## Step 3: Register for and Pass the RES Examination

### Exam Format

<cite index="23-1">The RES examination comprises two papers. Each paper has:
- Section A: 60 Multiple Choice Questions (60 marks)
- Section B: 20 MCQs with reference to one or two case studies (40 marks)</cite>

**Paper 1** covers legal aspects of property ownership, estate agency law, and property policies.

**Paper 2** covers practical aspects of property transactions — valuation, HDB procedures, private property purchases, and agency work.

### 2026 Exam Schedule

<cite index="22-1">Exams are currently held 3 times a year — February/March, June, and October.</cite> Register at least 2 months in advance as registration closes 1-2 months before each sitting.

<cite index="25-1">Due to high demand for RES examination seats, it will not be possible to allocate a seat to every applicant with a valid Certificate of RES Course Completion.</cite> Early registration is essential.

### Exam Fees

- Both papers (first sitting): approximately SGD 417-500
- Individual paper retake: approximately SGD 283 per paper
- NTUC members may be eligible for UTAP funding to offset fees

### If You Don't Pass

<cite index="25-1">If you fail one or both papers, you can retake individual papers at the next sitting. You must pass all papers within two years from the date of your Course Completion Certificate. If you don't pass within two years, you must retake the entire RES course and sit for both papers again.</cite>

The RES exam has a moderate difficulty level — the MCQ options are deliberately close, requiring genuine understanding rather than surface-level memorisation. Most candidates who study consistently for 4-8 weeks pass within 1-2 attempts.

## Step 4: Choose Your Agency and Register with CEA

### Selecting Your Agency

Once you pass the RES exam, you have **2 years** from the date of your Course Completion Certificate to register with an estate agency and apply to CEA. Do not leave this too long.

Your choice of agency is one of the most important decisions in your property agent career. It determines your training support, your team environment, your brand positioning, and — in the early years especially — your access to mentorship and resources.

The four largest agencies in Singapore by agent count are ERA, PropNex, Huttons, and OrangeTee & Tie. Each has a distinct culture and support structure (covered in detail in our agency comparison guide — link below).

Key questions to ask when evaluating agencies:
- What training and mentorship is provided in the first 12 months?
- What are the commission split arrangements, and how do they scale with performance?
- What marketing tools and technology platform do they provide?
- What is the team culture — individual performance focus or team-based support?
- Are there any upfront costs (insurance, CEA fees, technology subscriptions) that the agency subsidises?

Some agencies absorb initial costs like CEA registration fees and Professional Indemnity Insurance as part of their agent recruitment package. This is worth clarifying upfront — especially as a new agent managing early cash flow.

### CEA Registration Process

After selecting your agency, they will guide you through the CEA registration process:

1. Submit your RES Course Completion Certificate and exam results to CEA via your agency
2. CEA assesses your application — typically 4-6 weeks for In-Principle Approval (IPA)
3. Once approved, you are officially registered as a Real Estate Salesperson (RES)
4. CEA registration requires annual renewal

Annual renewal requirements include mandatory Continuing Professional Development (CPD) hours — agents must complete a minimum number of CPD training hours each year to maintain their registration.

## Step 5: The First 12 Months — What to Expect

### The Reality of Starting Out

Most new agents do not close their first transaction in the first month. The property agency career has a significant ramp-up period, and understanding this upfront prevents discouragement.

A realistic first-year timeline:

- **Month 1-2:** Learning your agency's systems, shadowing experienced agents, building your contact database, setting up your WhatsApp Business profile, completing mandatory new-agent training
- **Month 3-4:** First active listings or buyer mandates, possibly through family/friends network. First portal listings. Learning PropertyGuru and 99.co listing workflows.
- **Month 5-8:** First transaction likely in this window for most new agents with consistent effort. Building your first real testimonials.
- **Month 9-12:** Beginning to see referrals from early clients. Building a rhythm of 2-4 active leads at any time.

**The income gap is real:** Most new agents earn little or nothing in the first 3-6 months. This is normal — but you need to plan for it financially. The conventional advice is to have 6 months of personal expenses saved before going full-time.

### Part-Time vs Full-Time

<cite index="21-1">Some agents start as part-time. A few move to full-time when they feel more confident.</cite> Part-time is a legitimate starting point — particularly for agents with stable income from another profession who want to build a client base before making the full-time commitment.

The limitation of part-time: responsiveness to buyer and seller enquiries is a significant driver of conversion. Agents who cannot respond quickly during business hours lose leads to full-time competitors. Part-time works better in the rental segment and with buyer clients who have flexible timelines than in the seller-side listing market where speed of response matters.

### The Tools You Will Need from Day One

New agents often underestimate the importance of professional tools in the first year. Competing for the same listings and buyers as experienced agents means you need to present professionally from the start — even before you have a track record.

A practical starter toolkit:
- **WhatsApp Business** — set up with a professional profile, your niche, and your CEA registration number
- **PropertyGuru and 99.co** subscriptions — your primary listing platforms
- **Canva** — for social media graphics and property flyers (free tier is sufficient to start)
- **mypropfolio** — for creating branded listing microsites that make every listing look professional, regardless of how new you are. This is particularly valuable for new agents because it levels the presentation playing field — a buyer who receives a mypropfolio listing link has no way of knowing if the agent closed 0 transactions or 200.
- **A basic CRM** — HubSpot free tier is sufficient to start tracking your leads and follow-up schedule

### Building Your Niche from the Start

The most common mistake new agents make is trying to serve every property type in every district. The agents who build momentum fastest in their first year are the ones who pick a niche early — HDB resale in one or two estates, condos in a specific district, rentals in a specific area — and go deep on that niche rather than spreading thin across everything.

| RES Exam (both papers) | 417 - 500 |
| CEA Registration | ~350 (may be absorbed by agency) |
| Professional Indemnity Insurance | ~300/year (may be absorbed by agency) |

| mypropfolio subscription | Affordable monthly plan |

Many agencies absorb the CEA registration and insurance costs as part of agent recruitment. Portal subscription costs are the largest ongoing expense and scale with the number of active listings you manage.

## The Bottom Line

Becoming a property agent in Singapore is a structured process — eligibility check, RES course, exam, agency selection, and CEA registration — that takes approximately 4-6 months from start to first day on the job.

What comes after registration is less structured and more challenging: building a client base, developing market knowledge, and learning to present listings and advise clients professionally. The agents who build sustainable careers are not necessarily the ones with the most natural charisma or the biggest existing networks. They are the ones who approach the profession systematically — building niche expertise, professional tools, and consistent follow-up processes from the very beginning.
