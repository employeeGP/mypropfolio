# How to Brief Your Property Photographer for Maximum Results in Singapore

Professional property photography is only as good as the brief you give before the shoot and the preparation you do on the day. The most experienced property photographer in Singapore cannot compensate for a cluttered unit, poor timing, or unclear instructions about what the listing needs to communicate.

This guide covers exactly how to set up every shoot for maximum results — from booking to delivery.

---

## Why the Brief Matters as Much as the Photographer

<cite index="23-1">The photographer controls the camera; you control the room. Preparation has a bigger impact on the final images than almost anything else.</cite>

Most agents who are disappointed with their property photos have the same underlying problem: they booked a photographer, gave them the address, and showed up on shoot day without a plan.

A well-briefed photographer knows:
- Which rooms and features to prioritise
- What the property's strongest selling points are
- Who the likely buyer is and what they need to see
- Which areas to downplay or minimise
- What time of day the unit looks best

With this information, a good photographer can make strategic decisions about angles, lighting, and sequencing that a generic walkthrough approach will miss entirely.

---

## Step 1: Share the Listing Brief Before the Shoot Day

Send your photographer a short briefing note 24-48 hours before the shoot. This does not need to be long — a WhatsApp message or email with the following information is sufficient:

**Property details:**
- Address and floor number (floor affects natural light planning)
- Unit orientation / facing direction
- Property type (HDB 4-room resale / 2BR condo / landed semi-D etc.)

**Selling points to emphasise:**
List the 3-5 features that are most likely to convert a browser into a viewer. Examples:
- "The master bedroom has an unobstructed city view — this should be a hero shot"
- "The kitchen was renovated 2 years ago — capture the cabinetry and countertops clearly"
- "The unit is on a high floor with a good breeze — the balcony should feature prominently"
- "The pool view from the living room is the main selling point — this needs a wide shot from inside looking out"

**Buyer profile:**
Who are you marketing to? This affects which features deserve emphasis:
- HDB upgrader families → emphasise storage, bedroom count, proximity to schools
- Investor buyers → emphasise rental yield potential, low-maintenance condition
- Expat tenants → emphasise natural light, airiness, proximity to MRT
- Downsizers → emphasise single-level layout, low maintenance, community facilities

**Areas to handle carefully:**
Every property has something that is less than ideal — a small bathroom, a corridor-facing window, an older kitchen. Brief your photographer on how to handle these honestly: the goal is accurate and flattering, not misleading.

## Step 2: Prepare the Property the Day Before

<cite index="24-1">Decluttering is one of the simplest yet most effective tips for real estate photos for selling.</cite>

The seller brief for shoot preparation:

**Remove all personal items from visible surfaces:**
- Kitchen: clear countertops completely. Remove dish racks, small appliances (unless they are high-quality built-ins), magnets from the fridge door
- Bathrooms: remove all toiletries, toothbrushes, and personal items from counters and shower ledges
- Bedrooms: remove items from bedside tables, clear wardrobes if doors will be open
- Living room: remove remote controls, magazines, children's toys, and any items that break the visual clean

**Oversized or excess furniture:**
For compact HDB units especially, consider removing a piece of furniture temporarily if it makes the space feel cramped. A living room photographed with a 3-seater sofa, coffee table, and TV console will feel more spacious than the same room with an additional armchair wedged in.

**Lighting check:**
- Replace any blown bulbs — dead bulbs create uneven light that is difficult to correct in editing
- Ensure all ceiling lights are functioning
- Test any feature lighting (under-cabinet lighting in kitchen, feature lighting in living area) and have it ready to switch on

**Cleaning priorities before the shoot:**
- Glass surfaces (windows, mirror, glass table tops) — smudges are highly visible in wide-angle shots
- Floor surfaces — especially for open-plan units where the floor runs continuously through multiple spaces
- Bathroom tiles and fittings — grout lines and fixture condition are visible in professional photos
- Kitchen — stovetop, sink, and visible appliances should be spotless

---

## Step 3: On Shoot Day — What to Do and What to Avoid

**1-2 hours before the photographer arrives:**
- Open all curtains and blinds fully
- Switch on all artificial lighting — living room, dining room, kitchen, all bedrooms, bathrooms
- Do a final declutter sweep — remove anything that arrived after your preparation the day before
- Add simple staging touches: fresh flowers on the dining table, a folded throw on the sofa, a neat stack of books

**When the photographer arrives:**
- Walk them through the unit and point out the priority shots you discussed in the brief
- Show them the hero feature in person — sometimes describing a city view is less effective than standing at the window and seeing it together
- Confirm the sequence they plan to shoot in and raise any timing considerations (e.g., the balcony gets direct sun after 2pm, the living room is best in the morning)

**During the shoot:**
- Make yourself available but stay out of the frame and do not hover
- If the seller or tenant is present, manage them — people lingering in doorways or visible in mirrors create retouching work and slow down the shoot
- Check in after the key shots are done — ask to see the living room and master bedroom shots on the camera screen before the photographer moves on

**After the shoot:**
- <cite index="23-1">Confirm turnaround time — portal-ready edits within a day or two matter when a listing is time-sensitive.</cite> Most professional photographers deliver edited files within 1-2 business days; confirm this before the shoot rather than after.
- When you receive the files, review them against your brief before uploading to portals. Check that the hero shots match the selling points you identified, and that no unflattering angles have been included that misrepresent the property.

## Step 4: What to Do With the Photos Once You Have Them

Professional photography is only as effective as where and how you use it. Most agents upload the photos to PropertyGuru and stop there.

The agents getting the most value from their photography investment use it across multiple channels:

**Dedicated listing microsite:** A mypropfolio listing page displays your professional photos in full-width gallery format — not the compressed thumbnails of portal listings. Buyers who receive a microsite link see the photos at their best quality. When shared on WhatsApp, the hero shot previews correctly and creates a strong first impression before the buyer even opens the link.

**Social media:** Professional photos perform significantly better on Instagram and Facebook than phone photos. Use the hero shot as the cover for a property post and include the microsite link in the caption.

**WhatsApp sharing:** Send the microsite link rather than individual photos. A dedicated page with the full photo gallery, property specs, and your WhatsApp CTA converts better than a series of images sent directly in a chat.

**Pre-listing pack:** For your next listing appointment, include sample photos from your best previous shoots as evidence of how you present properties. A seller who sees the quality of your previous listing photography before signing the listing agreement has much higher confidence in your marketing approach.
