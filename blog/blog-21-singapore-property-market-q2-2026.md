# Singapore Property Market Update Q2 2026: What Agents Need to Know

The Q2 2026 data is in — and it tells two very different stories depending on which segment you are in. For HDB resale agents, the market is cooling more clearly than at any point since 2019. For private residential agents, particularly those in the Core Central Region and landed segment, conditions remain resilient despite a broader deceleration.

Here is a data-driven breakdown of where the market stands heading into the second half of 2026, and what it means for agent strategy.

---

## HDB Resale Market: Second Consecutive Quarterly Decline

<cite index="30-1">According to HDB's flash estimates, resale flat prices edged down for the second consecutive quarter. Following the 0.1% quarter-on-quarter decline in Q1 2026, the HDB Resale Price Index slipped a further 0.3% in Q2 2026 to 202.7. This marks its first back-to-back decline since the four consecutive quarter-over-quarter declines from Q3 2018 to Q2 2019.</cite>

**Key Q2 2026 HDB numbers:**
- HDB Resale Price Index: 202.7 (down 0.3% QoQ, down 0.4% for 1H 2026 cumulatively)
- Total Q2 2026 resale transactions: 6,268 units (down 0.3% from Q1's 6,285)
- 1H 2026 total resale volume: 12,553 units — <cite index="30-1">8.3% lower year-over-year than the 13,692 units sold in 1H 2025</cite>
- Million-dollar flat transactions in Q2 2026: 491 additional deals, bringing 1H 2026 total to 902 — <cite index="30-1">surpassing the 763 recorded in 1H 2025</cite>

**What is driving the softening:**

<cite index="30-1">Cooling measures such as the 15-month wait-out period for private property sellers, together with larger BTO supply, have eased pressure on resale HDB prices since the start of 2025.</cite> More directly, <cite index="30-1">a total of 13,480 units are set to reach MOP in 2026, 93.3% more than the 6,973 units in 2025</cite> — nearly doubling the supply of newly eligible resale flats entering the market simultaneously.

**The divergence within HDB resale:**

The index decline masks a significant split. <cite index="32-1">The RPI dip masks a divergence: million-dollar flats kept rising while standard 4-room and 3-room mass-market resale eased.</cite> Mature estate premium units in Bukit Merah, Toa Payoh, and Queenstown continued transacting at record levels, while non-mature estate mass-market flats faced more downward pressure on price and longer days on market.

**Full-year HDB outlook:**

<cite index="30-1">In 2026, ERA expects the HDB resale market to see around 26,000 to 27,000 transactions, with annual price growth ranging between 2% and 5%.</cite> PropNex similarly projects 26,000 to 27,000 total transactions for the year. The consensus view is a market finding equilibrium rather than declining — with two soft quarters followed by stabilisation in 2H 2026 as MOP supply is gradually absorbed.

## Private Residential Market: Diverging by Segment

<cite index="38-1">The Urban Redevelopment Authority released the flash estimate for Q2 2026 on 1 July 2026. The headline number — a +0.5% quarter-on-quarter increase — represents a notable deceleration from the +0.9% recorded in Q1 2026.</cite>

**Key Q2 2026 private residential numbers:**
- Overall PPI: +0.5% QoQ (vs +0.9% in Q1 2026)
- Core Central Region (CCR): <cite index="38-1">+2.0% QoQ — the strongest performing segment</cite>
- Rest of Central Region (RCR): -1.4% QoQ — weakest performing segment
- Outside Central Region (OCR): -0.2% QoQ (vs +2.2% in Q1 2026)
- Landed properties: <cite index="38-1">+2.6% QoQ — a sharp reversal from -0.4% in Q1 2026</cite>
- New home sales (Q2 2026): 2,116 units, up 5.1% from Q1's 2,013 units
- 1H 2026 new sales total: 4,129 units — <cite index="41-1">11.1% lower than the 4,587 units sold in 1H 2025</cite>

**The CCR surge:**

<cite index="41-1">Developers have also become increasingly bullish in CCR Government Land Sale tenders that closed in the quarter — in particular, Peck Hay Road, Dunearn Road (2) and River Valley Parcel C, which recorded benchmark bid prices above comparable 2025 GLS tenders in the same location.</cite> The luxury end of the market is attracting both local and foreign capital despite the broader deceleration.

**The OCR pullback and its connection to HDB:**

<cite index="40-1">The slight decline in OCR prices could be attributed to Tengah Garden Residences (863 units), the first private condo launch in Tengah, which moved 853 units or 99% of its total units at an average price of $2,120 psf. This also coincides with the HDB resale price index falling for a second consecutive quarter, down a cumulative 0.4% in 1H 2026, which may be indicative of weaker upgrading power.</cite>

The logic is straightforward: OCR condos are the primary upgrade destination for HDB sellers. When HDB resale prices soften, sellers realise less from their flat — reducing their effective budget for the upgrade. This compresses OCR demand at the margin.

**Full-year private residential outlook:**

<cite index="40-1">CBRE Research expects 7,500 to 8,500 new homes to be sold in 2026, a moderation from the high base of 10,815 units in 2025. Private home prices, which have risen 1.4% in 1H 2026, could grow at a similar pace in 2H 2026, maintaining the full-year forecast of 2% to 4% growth.</cite>

## Supply: The Dominant Story for 2H 2026

Both markets are being shaped primarily by supply rather than demand shifts.

**HDB supply:** <cite index="30-1">Non-urgent buyers prioritising affordability may also pivot toward the BTO market, especially with popular locations being offered. The upcoming October BTO exercise, the largest of the year, will feature 7,970 units across six locations, including standout projects at Toa Payoh West and Bayshore.</cite>

**Private residential supply:** <cite index="46-1">In 2H 2026, 4,745 private residential units will be launched under the Confirmed List, bringing the full-year Confirmed List supply to 9,320 units — over 50% higher than the past 10-year annual average. Around 61,000 private residential units (including ECs) are expected to be completed in the next few years.</cite>

The government's high-supply stance is deliberate and sustained. <cite index="46-1">URA specifically noted that the macroeconomic outlook remains highly uncertain and urged households to exercise prudence when purchasing property and taking out mortgage loans.</cite>

## What This Means for Agents

**For HDB resale specialists:**

The market is becoming more price-sensitive. <cite index="32-1">Listings priced ahead of valuation are sitting longer; price-to-value listings still clear within standard timeframes. Cash-Over-Valuation bidding has compressed substantially in non-mature estates.</cite>

The agent conversation with sellers needs to shift accordingly: realistic pricing and professional marketing quality become more important in a softening market than in the post-pandemic frenzy, when almost anything sold quickly at asking. Sellers who understand the MOP supply context — and price accordingly — will transact faster.

**For private condo agents:**

The market is bifurcating. CCR and landed specialists are operating in a strong segment; RCR and OCR agents need to manage both seller expectations (especially on older resale stock competing with well-priced new launches) and buyer patience (buyers have more options than 12 months ago).

**For agents across all segments:**

In a market where price growth is slowing and buyer selectivity is increasing, professional listing presentation becomes a differentiator. Buyers with more time and more options will spend longer evaluating each property — which means the quality of your photos, the comprehensiveness of your listing information, and the professionalism of how you present the property all have more impact on enquiry conversion than in a hot market where anything generates interest.

A dedicated listing microsite with professional photography, full specs, floor plan, and a branded agent profile is not just a marketing nicety in 2H 2026 — it is increasingly the standard that serious buyers expect before they will commit to a viewing.

## The Bottom Line for Agents

Q2 2026 confirms what Q1 hinted at: Singapore's property market is entering a period of measured normalisation after three years of post-pandemic heat. This is not a correction — volumes remain healthy, and the government's economic management provides a stable backdrop — but it is a market that rewards preparation, pricing accuracy, and professional presentation more than momentum did.

For agents who present their listings well, communicate market realities clearly to sellers, and serve buyers with comprehensive pre-viewing information, a stabilising market is an opportunity to stand out from the majority who are still operating on post-pandemic autopilot.
